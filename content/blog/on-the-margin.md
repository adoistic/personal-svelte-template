The first thing nobody tells you about writing a novel is how much of it happens in the margins of other lives.

For most of the three years I spent on _The Quiet Sky_, I was also working a library shift, paying rent in a city that didn't want me to, and reading other people's books with what energy I had left. The novel grew in the gaps — on the subway, in the half-hour before bed, in the slow Tuesday afternoons when the reference desk was quiet and I could keep a notebook open under the counter without anyone noticing.

I used to think this was the wrong way to do it. The romantic image of the novelist — Annie Dillard in the cabin, Toni Morrison waking at 4 AM — always implied that real work needed a real space. A room of one's own, scaled up to a writing life of one's own.

But I don't think that's true. Or rather: I think it's true for some people, and a trap for others. For me, the margins were the work. The novel got better the more it had to compete for my attention. The scenes that survived were the ones I couldn't stop thinking about during the parts of my life that weren't writing.

I'm thinking about this because someone wrote to me last week asking how to start. They have a job. They have kids. They have, in their words, "no time." And I wanted to tell them: the no-time is the time. The constraint is the gift. You're going to write the kind of novel that someone in a cabin couldn't, because you can't help it. The novel will be made of the life you already have.

This isn't a productivity argument. I'm not saying everyone should hustle. I'm saying — and this took me years to learn — that you don't need to clear a space to begin. The space is already there, in the margins. You just have to start writing in it.
