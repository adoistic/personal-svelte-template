The last hour at the library was always the most interesting one.

By 7 PM the parents with strollers had gone home. The afternoon retirees had drifted out around five. What was left, on a typical Tuesday, was: one or two college students working on something due tomorrow, one person who clearly didn't have anywhere else to be, and — almost every night — a man named Reggie who came in to use the computer.

Reggie didn't have email. He had a number written on a piece of paper that he wanted me to call. I would not call it. He understood this. He would sit at the computer anyway, sometimes for the whole hour, sometimes for ten minutes, and then he'd leave with a small nod that I came to understand meant _see you tomorrow._

I think about him a lot. Not because of the obvious reasons — the loneliness, the social safety net, the way public space holds people who have nowhere else. All of that is true and worth thinking about. But what I actually think about is the nod. The fact that a stranger I never quite spoke to was, for two years, the most consistent presence in my evenings.

A library is one of the few places where you can be alone without being alone. I don't know any other building in a city that does this. Coffee shops want you to buy something. Bars want you to drink. Parks turn dangerous after dark. The library is just — there. With its lights on. Until 8 PM. With a person at the desk who, no, won't make a call for you, but who will recognize your face when you come back tomorrow.

If you live somewhere with a public library, go to it. Not because you need a book. Just so it's there for the next person who does.
